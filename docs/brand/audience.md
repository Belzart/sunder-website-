# Audience

## Priority Order

1. **Investors** — should feel this is a fundable, visionary company
2. **Future customers / partners** — should feel this technology could solve real problems
3. **Technical talent** — should feel this is where they want to work

## What Each Audience Needs

### Investors
- **Feel**: "This team sees something no one else does."
- **Need**: Credibility signals, vision clarity, category creation, taste
- **Risk to avoid**: Looking like a science project with no business gravity
- **Key moment**: First 10 seconds. The blob + environment must signal "real company, real ambition" before any text is read.

### Customers / Partners
- **Feel**: "This material could solve problems we didn't know were solvable."
- **Need**: Application hints, technical seriousness, contact path
- **Risk to avoid**: Over-promising specific capabilities too early
- **Key moment**: The domains/applications section. Must feel grounded and specific, not handwavy.

### Technical Talent
- **Feel**: "These people are building something I want to be part of."
- **Need**: Technical depth signals, interaction quality, craftsmanship in the build itself
- **Risk to avoid**: Looking like a marketing exercise with no substance
- **Key moment**: The interaction quality of the site itself IS the recruiting pitch. If the site is technically impressive, the right people will notice.

## What All Audiences Share

- They should feel curiosity first, not information overload
- They should feel the company is further along than it reveals
- They should want to be involved before it becomes public
- They should leave with the site stuck in their memory

## Impersonal Presentation

- No people, faces, or founders visible early in the experience
- The artifact (material) is the protagonist
- The lab/world is the setting
- Human presence emerges only deeper in the experience, if at all
